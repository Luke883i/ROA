Abstract
This annex specifies a fully computable Reticular Local Abstraction (RLA) topology for a
generalist bryophyte. The topology is compact in the RLA/CRC sense: all relevant entities
and rules are internal to the lattice (Internal Ontological Autonomy, IOA), predictions are
produced without appeal to extra–reticular information (Epistemic Closure, EC), and every
rule is algorithmic (Turing–Computability, TC). The package includes: (i) twenty abstraction
levels (L01–L20); (ii) forty–two parameters (P01–P42) with units, literature ranges and
operational reconduction rules; (iii) families of update equations and causal relations; (iv)
threshold actions and rare–event injectors; (v) collapse / emergence metrics and composite
indices; (vi) climate scenarios, initial state, halting / success and an adaptive mechanism. The
bryophyte system is treated as anepistemicmodel: its levels, variables and equations encode
disciplined scientific knowledge about the domain, without claiming to be an ultimate ontology
of life. Synthetic trajectories generated by this topology are intended to be epistemically
indistinguishable, under blind expert inspection, from empirical observations collected under
analogous conditions.

1 Introduction
Multi–level biological systems display macro–regularities that are not transparently reducible to
micro–details (Anderson, 1972; Kitano, 2002; Godfrey–Smith, 2007). Bryophytes are a useful
paradigm: from genomic regulation and chromatin state, through hormone and ROS dynamics,
to tissue mechanics, demography and community interactions (Glime, 2007; Proctor, 2000, 2011).
Reticular Local Abstraction (RLA) represents such systems as lattices of levels linked
by explicit transmission functions. Non–injective transmissions implement principled coarse–
grainings and can support irreducible macro–properties; quasi–injective transmissions, when
present, allow Turing–level undecidability to propagate upward in a controlled way.
In the terminology of Annex A, the structure constructed here is intended to be a compact
reticulum satisfying Internal Ontological Autonomy (IOA), Epistemic Closure (EC) and Turing–
Computability (TC), and therefore a concrete instance of Compact Reticular Computability
(CRC) for a natural system. This annex provides acompactRLA topology for a generalist
bryophyte: all explanatory work and all predictive power remain internal to the lattice, and the
dynamics are fully Turing–computable. The lattice is designed as a computational reference
case that can later be mapped to epistemic architectures (e.g. ECNNs) and to more abstract
discussions on computability and emergence.

2.4.7 Causal relations
Equations (2)–(26) define a causal graphG across all parameters. Seasonal forcing drives
temperature, light and rainfall; these drive water and ABA; water, stress and temperature shape
ROS, antioxidants and photosynthesis; nutrients and pH modulate photosynthesis; hormones,
stress and senescence determine vitality and reproduction; clonal structure feeds back into
composite metrics. All maps are internal to the levels of Section 2.1.

5 Compactness and epistemic indistinguishability
5.1 Compact RLA/CRC properties
LetL ={L01,...,L 20}andT =⟨L,{fij},Σ⟩be the bryophyte lattice, wherefij are transmission
functions between levels andΣis the finite set of update rules (equations, actions, rare events,
metrics).
Internal Ontological Autonomy (IOA).All state variablesP 01–P 42live on levels inL;
all update rules inΣmanipulate only these variables. Exogenous drivers (climate forcings) are
represented via internal parameters at L09–L10. No rule refers to entities outside the lattice.
Manyfij are deliberately non–injective, collapsing microstates into aggregated macro–states (e.g.
vitality, desiccation indices), thereby preventing external undecidability from leaking in.
Epistemic Closure (EC).For any proposition about the system (e.g. “vitality will stay above
50 % over the next 100 days under scenarioS”), truth within the model is decidable by iterated
application of the internal prediction operatorPt induced byΣ. No external oracle, dataset or
law is required. All observational predicates refer to variables at levels L01–L20.
Turing–Computability (TC).Each element ofΣis a composition of Turing–computable
functions on rationally representable reals. Thus, for any finite horizonT∈Nand any initial
stateP(0), there exists a finite algorithm that computesP(1) ,..., P(T). This suffices for
Turing–computability for all practical purposes.
The bryophyte lattice is therefore a compact reticulum in the sense of Compact Reticular
Computability (CRC).
5.2 Epistemic indistinguishability
Definition (epistemic indistinguishability).Let S be a fixed set of observables and let
O be an observer restricted toS. A real trajectoryτreal and a simulated trajectoryτsim are
epistemically indistinguishable forO at thresholdηif, given only the restricted outputsτreal|S

## Page 16

and τsim|S, no decision procedure available toO can assign which trajectory is real with success
probability strictly greater thanη.
In the bryophyte setting,S might contain only a subset of variables (e.g. biomass proxies,
fluorescence, coarse stress indices) observed at finite temporal resolution. If an expert observer
O restricted toS cannot reliably distinguish real from synthetic trajectories above a chosen
threshold η(e.g. η= 0.7), then, relative toS and η, the compact RLA topology is empirically
adequate in the sense of this definition.
Under the RLA view, a compact topology can in principle produce synthetic time series of
observables that are epistemically indistinguishable from empirical data, provided that:
1. the micro–foundation (levels, parameters, ranges) is consistent with current biological
knowledge;
2. the observation protocol for the simulation matches that of real experiments (same variables,
aggregation rules and noise);
3. the expert observer has no access to extra–lattice information about data provenance.
A blind test proceeds as follows:
• generate simulated trajectories of key observables (e.g. proxies of biomass, fluorescence,
stress indices) under a chosen climate scenario;
•collect real trajectories from controlled microcosms under comparable conditions;
•mix and anonymise both sets and present them to a bryophyte expert;
• ask the expert to classify each series as “real” or “synthetic” and compare performance
with chance.
If no statistically significant discrimination is possible, this does not prove ontological identity
between simulation and reality. However, it supports the claim that the compact RLA topology
captures sufficient structure of the system to reproduce its observable dynamics in the chosen
domain of discourse. This constitutes a falsifiable prediction of the framework and an analogue
of a Turing test for empirical vs. computed trajectories.
6 Discussion and limitations
The compact RLA topology for a generalist bryophyte illustrates how a non–trivial biological
system can be represented as a finite, multi–level computational object with clear semantics and
explicit closure properties.
Key points:
• The design is intentionallyepistemic. Levels and parameters encode a discretised scientific
understanding of the organism, not an ultimate ontology. Empirical ranges and rules are
drawn from the literature but remain revisable.
16

## Page 17

• Non–injective transmissions are used as constructive design tools: they encode how scientists
legitimately aggregate micro–details into macro–indices (e.g. vitality, stress, desiccation).
Irreversible information loss at these interfaces explains how emergent properties can be
both real (for the model) and computationally manageable.
• Collapse and emergence metrics provide a bridge from dynamical systems analysis (critical
transitions, early–warning signals) to practical diagnostics for the bryophyte, and more
generally, for complex systems with similar multi–level structure.
Limitations include the absence of explicit spatial heterogeneity, simplified treatment of
species differences, and uncertainty in many parameter estimates. Nonetheless, the topology
is rich enough to support stress tests, scenario analysis and epistemic experiments about the
boundary between simulated and empirical realities.
Sensitivity (conceptual)
The present topology is not calibrated in a strict statistical sense, but its structure makes it
possible to reason qualitatively about sensitivity and robustness. A few parameters and thresholds
play a critical role:
• Stress accumulation and relief (P10 and related gains).Increasing the gains
kp,k h,k t in Eq. (17) makes cumulative stress grow faster for the same ROS, drought
and heat patterns. Trajectories cross the collapse metricsMP11,crashand MP34,critmore
often, and the lattice exhibits earlier and more frequent transitions to low–vitality regimes.
Decreasing these gains has the opposite effect and extends the basin of attraction of
high–vitality trajectories.
• Climate forcing (parameters forP14 andP31).Raising Tmean,AT or the warming trend
rtrend in Eqs.(2)–(6) increases the frequency of excursions above the thermal thresholds
in the stress and ROS equations. In RLA terms, transmissions from climate levels (L09–
L10) to physiological levels (L03–L04) become more strongly non–injective: a larger
fraction of distinct climate microstates collapses onto similar “overstressed” physiological
configurations, reducing resilience.
• Water status (P35, P36).Modifying ks,k e,k d in Eqs. (7)–(8) changes how rapidly
surface and deep water respond to rainfall and heat. Largerke or smallerks sharpen
drought episodes at L09 and L13 into more frequent activation of threshold actions such as
A_DES_0, effectively strengthening the collapse from water dynamics to photosynthesis
and vitality. Conversely, parameter choices that maintainP35 and P36 in intermediate
ranges widen the set of micro–trajectories that remain in high–vitality macro–states.
• Vitality non–linearity (P11).The slope and threshold parameters in Eq.(18) control
how sharply vitality responds to fitness and stress. A steeper sigmoidal function turns
moderate increases inP10 into abrupt drops inP11, making collapse metrics more sensitive
and increasing the reticular irreducibility index: more of the lattice must be taken into
account to anticipate tipping points.

• Epigenetic buffering (P16, P18).The rules in the adaptive mechanism section couple
high stress (P10) to increases in P16 and P18, which in turn reduce the thermal ROS gain
in Eq.(10). Strengthening this feedback (larger increments in P16, P18) can delay or even
prevent collapse for certain climate scenarios, effectively weakening the collapse of climate
trajectories onto a small set of stressed physiological states. Weakening the feedback has
the opposite effect and makes the lattice more fragile to small changes in forcing.
From an RLA/CRC perspective, these qualitative sensitivities can be read as statements
about how the non–injective transmissions between climate, physiology and systemic levels
change with parameters. Stronger non–injective collapse at critical interfaces (e.g. L09–L10→
L03–L04, L12→L20) corresponds to reduced resilience and more frequent entry into collapse
metrics; parameter regimes that maintain a richer variety of mesoscopic states at these interfaces
correspond to wider basins of attraction for healthy regimes.
